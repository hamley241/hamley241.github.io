Startup to AWS: Lessons from Both Worlds

February 10, 2026 · 10 min read · Career, Distributed Systems, Engineering Culture

I went from a startup with 100M users to AWS managing over 1 million instances.

Here's what surprised me: it changed everything I thought I knew about building software.

The Startup: Move Fast and Ship Things

My first real scale experience was at Hike Messenger — one of India's largest messaging apps with over 100 million users.

We were young, hungry, and fast. Really fast.

One week, we decided to build a dating feature backend. Tight deadline. High stakes. One week to ship.

We launched it.

Another time, we shipped India's first UPI payment feature on a chat messenger — before WhatsApp, before everyone else. Speed was our competitive advantage.

Our philosophy? "Move fast, fix things later."

And you know what? It worked. For that stage. For that pressure. For that competition.

But it came at a cost.

The AWS Reality Check

When I joined AWS Lambda and then the BigMac (Amazon EC2 large fleet) team, the scale numbers stopped being impressive and started being terrifying:

  • Over 1 million instances under management
  • Billions of requests processed daily
  • A single wrong config could impact millions of customers

Here, "move fast and fix things later" isn't bravery. It's negligence.

At hyperscale, every shortcut compounds. Every "we'll fix it later" becomes "we're paying for this decision every day."

I learned my first hard lesson: speed without reliability is just chaos waiting to happen.

Lesson 1: Automation Isn't Optional — It's Survival

The 40-Minute Problem

When I started on the health systems team at AWS, incident response took 40 minutes from alert to full mitigation.

Not because the engineers were slow. Not because they weren't talented. But because humans were in the critical path of every decision.

Here's how it went:

  1. Alert fires (minute 0)
  2. On-call engineer wakes up (minute 5)
  3. Engineer assesses the situation (minute 15)
  4. Engineer decides on mitigation (minute 25)
  5. Engineer executes runbook (minute 35)
  6. System recovers (minute 40)

40 minutes. Every step was reasonable. The process was the problem.

We asked: what if the system could decide and act?

We built automated AZ weight-away systems with multi-signal detection, slow controlled responses, and built-in safeguards. The system could detect, decide, and mitigate — while keeping humans in the loop for oversight, not for execution.

The result: 40 minutes → 5 minutes.

Not because we worked faster. Because we worked smarter.

Lesson 2: "Works on My Machine" Doesn't Scale

At Hike, we'd often say "it works on my machine" and ship. At AWS, that's the first warning sign of a coming disaster.

Here's the uncomfortable truth about scale:

What works for 1,000 instances will break at 100,000.
What works at 100,000 will break at 1,000,000.

Each order of magnitude exposes new failure modes. The hidden assumptions in your design become the obvious bottlenecks of tomorrow.

At AWS, we design for the scale we haven't hit yet. We ask:

  • What happens when this grows 10x?
  • What's the blast radius if this fails?
  • Can we roll back in seconds, not minutes?

Scalability isn't about handling current load. It's about handling future load without redesign.

Lesson 3: The Best Feature Is the One Users Never Notice

In a startup, we celebrated launches. New features meant demos, screenshots, and press releases. Visibility is success.

At AWS, I learned something different:

The best infrastructure work is invisible.

Health systems, grey failure detection, automated recovery — users don't see these. But they feel it when they're missing.

I remember the first time our automated health system caught a grey failure (a silent degradation) and mitigated it before any customer noticed. No alert. No pager. No incident report.

Just logs showing the system protecting itself.

That was better than any launch announcement.

Infrastructure work is user experience work, just at a different layer.

Lesson 4: The Mindset Shift

Here's the summary of my entire journey:

Startup Mindset Hyperscale Mindset
"How fast can we ship?" "How do we ship fast AND sleep at night?"
Celebrate features Celebrate absence of incidents
Fix on failure Prevent failure entirely
Scale reactively Design proactively
Heroic firefighting Boring operations (the best kind)

Neither is wrong. Both matter. The best engineers know when to optimize for which.

What I Took With Me

Interestingly, my startup experience made me a better AWS engineer, and my AWS experience made me appreciate what startups accomplish.

From Hike, I brought:

  • Product sense — understanding what users actually need, not just what the spec says
  • Ruthless prioritization — when everything is on fire, you learn to focus
  • The UPI experience — cross-functional coordination, regulatory compliance, building under ambiguity

From AWS, I learned:

  • Data-driven decisions — opinions are cheap, metrics reveal truth
  • Automate or suffer — if you do it twice manually, you should automate it
  • Design for failure — assume everything will break, build accordingly

The Insight That Changed Everything

Humans should design the response. Systems should execute it.

We went from "engineer follows runbook" to "engineer writes the automation that follows the runbook."

Our job isn't to be fast at 3am. Our job is to make 3am incidents boring.

That's the real difference I experienced moving from a startup to AWS. Not the tools. Not the processes. The mindset that reliability is a feature, not an afterthought.

Conclusion: Two Worlds, One Goal

Whether you're shipping a dating feature in a week at a startup or protecting a million-instance fleet at AWS, the goal is the same: create value for users.

The startup taught me speed and creativity.
AWS taught me reliability and scale.

Both matter. The magic is knowing when to trade speed for reliability, and when reliability enables even greater speed.

The engineers who master both? They're the ones who build things that matter.
